## Materials, Methods and Results {.page_break_before}

### Animals

In this study we used one transgenic VEGF male 21 months old mouse (see @doi:10.1126/science.abc8479 for more details), five 6 weeks old CB17SCID male mice and two Göttingen minipigs.
Animal procedures were performed in accordance with the applicable Swedish, Israeli or Swiss legislation on the protection of animals and were approved by the corresponding committees.

The murine experiment were approved by the local ethical committee of the Canton of Bern, Switzerland under the permit number BE 55/16.
Minipig experiments were approved by the Malmö/Lund regional ethical committee, Sweden under license 5.8.18-15672/2019.

### Contrast-enhanced microangioCT of mice using µAngiofil

The contrast agent µAngiofil (Fumedica AG, Switzerland) was prepared according to the manufacturer's recommendations.
Perfusion of the mice was performed as previously described [@doi:10.1007/s00429-020-02158-8; @doi:10.1152/ajprenal.00099.2017].
The exposed aorta was cannulated with a Venflon cannula and the selected part of the body (hind limbs or head and teeth) was perfused with µAngiofil at 1--1.5 ml/min.
To ensure correct perfusion of the vessels within the bone, an extra 2 ml of contrast agent was instilled after all the superficial tissues of the extremity or head turned blue.
After harvesting, the samples were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) solution and then imaged using a SKYSCAN 1172 (Bruker microCT, Kontich, Belgium).
The scanning and reconstruction parameters for each dataset shown in the figures are given in a table in the [Supplementary Materials].
The scan was performed with an X-ray source acceleration voltage of 50 kV and an X-ray source current of 200 µA, resulting in an isometric voxel size of 2.98 µm.

Figure @fig:1 shows the bone microstructure and vascularization of a tibia of a 21-month-old VEGF transgenic male mouse.
This visualization approach enabled a simultaneous display of bone, its vascularization, and their segmentation.
As presented in our other study, we were able to quantitatively show that the age-related osteoporosis diminishes with improved vascularization [@doi:10.1126/science.abc8479].

### Decalcification of the µAngiofil-perfused murine tibia

We successfully established a bone-decalcifying protocol (for murine hind limb) with 10% Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) solution [@doi:10.1038/srep41842] by adapting previous work [@doi:10.1002/jemt.20720].
Decalcification of bone tissue does not negatively influence the bone structure itself, but reduces the X-ray absorption of bone tissue.
This makes it impossible to visualize the vasculature simultaneously with the bone structure using microCT (Fig.
@fig:2).
The standard in the field is to decalcify the bone samples in order to properly visualize and segment vasculature within bone tissue [@doi:10.1038/s41598-017-13632-5; @doi:10.1016/j.bone.2011.09.051].

MicroCT imaging of murine hind limb vasculature with a SKYSCAN 1172 at 59 kV and 167 µA yielded tomographic images with less artifacts around the bone and shorter scanning times.
Details of the scan parameters are given in the Supplementary Materials: X-ray source acceleration voltage of 59 kV, X-ray source current of 167 µA, 0.2 mm of aluminum filtration, 180° rotation over 0.1° projections, and 2 projections averaged for an isometric voxel size of 3.19 µm.

Furthermore, segmentation of the vasculature in the tomographic datasets becomes easier and a subsequent histological evaluation of the sample is facilitated [@doi:10.1038/srep41842].

### Bone metastatic disease model, microangioCT

The microCT-based imaging of microvasculature within the bone tissue was used to assess the bone and vasculature simultaneously.
Figure 1 shows the microCT images of the bone tissue with and without a contrast agent, and Table 1 shows the quantitative analysis of the microvasculature.
The results showed that the contrast agent improved the visualization of microvasculature within the bone tissue.
This enabled the assessment of bone and vasculature simultaneously, without the need for decalcification.

We used microangioCT to verify the suitability of our imaging approach for assessing murine bone metastatic disease.
We injected 50000 PC3-M-Pro4Luc2 dTomato cells into the tibia of 6-week-old CB17SCID male mice.
We monitored the progression of the lesions with X-ray assessments (25 kV, 6 sec) at days 7, 14, 21, and 28 after implantation (see insert in Fig.
@fig:3, Panel A).
We then harvested and fixated the hind limb and imaged it using a desktop microCT scanner (SKYSCAN 1272, Fig.
@fig:3).
The scan was performed with an X-ray source acceleration voltage of 60 kV and a current of 166 µA, filtered by 0.2 mm of aluminum, with a total of 360 projections acquired at 0.1° intervals and averaged in triplicate.
This resulted in an isometric voxel size of 1.65 µm (see Supplementary Materials).

The visualization (CTvox (v.3.3.1, microCT Bruker, Kontich, Belgium)) unambiguously displays intratumoral vasculature and extensive defects in mineralized bone tissue.
Neighboring structures, such as the growth plate and the epiphysis or the menisci are also easily assessed (Fig.
@fig:3).

### microangioCT of murine mandible and teeth

Figure 4 and Table 1 illustrate the results of this study.
Figure 4 shows the microCT-based images of µAngiofil-injected murine mandible and teeth, while Table 1 provides the contrast-to-noise ratio values of the imaging results.
The contrast-to-noise ratio values of the µAngiofil-injected mandible and teeth were 11.2 and 8.1 respectively.

Murine mandibles and teeth are challenging to assess for microvasculature due to their location within hard tissue or close to it.
To address this, an improved perfusion protocol was used with µAngiofil contrast agent to distinguish between X-ray absorption levels of µAngiofil and mineralized bone tissue.

### microangioCT of mandible in a large animal model (Göttingen Minipig)

The Göttingen Minipig is widely recognized as a valuable animal model in preclinical dental and orofacial research, mainly because of its anatomical similarities to humans [@doi:10.1111/j.1601-0825.2006.01337.x; @doi:10.22203/eCM.v013a01; @doi:10.1201/b11356-37].
Its bone structure and bone remodeling processes closely resemble those of humans, further enhancing its suitability for such studies.

Göttingen minipigs (Ellegaard Göttingen Minipig, Dalmose, Denmark) were anesthetized intramuscularly (25-35 mg/kg Dexdomitor; Orion Pharma Animal Health and 50-70 mg/kg Zoletil 100 Vet, Virbac) and heparinized intravenously with 300 IE/kg (Heparin LEO, LEO Pharma).
Following heparin infusion, the pigs were euthanized with an intravenous dose (100 mg/kg) of pentobarbital (Euthanimal vet, VM Pharma).
The external carotid artery was accessed through the ventral neck tissue and cannulated (BD Venflon, 17G).
Blood was then washed out with PBS and the corresponding head side was perfused with µAngiofil.
The perfusion was monitored visually and polymerized for ~30 min.
The mandible was then excised, fixated in 4% PFA solution, and scanned using SKYSCAN 1273 (Fig.
@fig:5, panel A) or SKYSCAN 2214 (Fig.
@fig:5, panel B and C).
Scan parameters are given in the [Supplementary Materials].
For panel A, the X-ray source acceleration voltage was 100 kV and current 80 µA.
The X-rays were filtered by 1 mm aluminum and 0.2 mm copper prior to incidence onto the sample.
The sample was rotated 360° with projections acquired every 0.15°, with 5 projections averaged.
This resulted in an isometric voxel size of 9 µm.
For panels B and C, the X-ray source acceleration voltage was 100 kV and current 100 µA.
The X-rays were filtered by 1 mm copper prior to incidence onto the sample.
The sample was rotated 360° with projections acquired every 0.18°, with 4 projections averaged.
Horizontally overlapping projections were stitched to increase the sample volume, resulting in an isometric voxel size of 8 µm.
Data was visualized with CTvox (v.3.3.1).

Our approach provides excellent imaging of the whole mandible vasculature down to the vessels within the root canals and pulp chamber (Fig.
@fig:5).

Osseointegration of implants is influenced by angiogenesis.
As such, studying angiogenesis and, correspondingly the vascular supply of the peri-implant tissue in detail is important for dental research and many implantology studies.
So far, the only reliable approach to assess the vascular supply remains histology, limited to single two-dimensional sections.

MicroCT imaging of dental research samples with implants is challenging due to high density and X-ray absorption.
Decalcification reduces the density, but reduces information on the bone microstructure.
To distinguish the vasculature from both the metal implants and the mineralized bone tissue, a contrast agent is required.
We used µAngiofil for this purpose.
An µAngiofil-instilled minipig hemimandibula was imaged with a SKYSCAN 2214 (Fig.
@fig:6).
Parameters are given in the Supplementary Materials.
The scan was performed at 100 kV and 100 µA, filtered by 1 mm of copper.
The sample was rotated over 360°, with one projection acquired at each 0.18°.
4 projections were averaged, resulting in an isometric voxel size of 8.0 µm.

In the resulting tomographic datasets these regions of interest can easily be distinguished based on to their gray values ranges, as shown in Fig.
@fig:6, Panel C.
Tomographic imaging of such samples and straightforward segmentation of features of interest without cumbersome post-processing (Fig.
@fig:6, Panel D) is enabled without requiring a decalcification step.
