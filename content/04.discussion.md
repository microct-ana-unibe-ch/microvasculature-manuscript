## Discussion {.page_break_before}

Adequate vascularization is essential for successful bone formation and regeneration, as well as osseointegration of biomaterials [@doi:10.1021/acsabm.1c00979; @doi:10.1089/ten.teb.2020.0132; @doi:10.1242/dev.136861; @doi:10.1002/jbm.a.32411; @doi:10.1042/CS20180155].
Without high-resolution imaging, it is difficult to understand the relationship between vasculature and bone tissue, making it impossible to control these processes.
Non-destructive microCT imaging is the only viable approach for 3D imaging of an intact bone-implant interface [@doi:10.1111/cpr.12578].
To distinguish the vasculature from other soft tissue structures, a contrast agent must be instilled or an intravascular casting must be produced.
However, without intravascular casting or contrast agents, the microCT-based distinction of the vessels from other soft tissue structures is not possible.
Our method of imaging the microvasculature within bones based on instillation with µAngiofil overcomes several drawbacks of previously described methods (for details, see [Introduction] for a thorough explanation) and is suitable for the visualization of the microvasculature within bone tissue with or without decalcification (Fig.
@fig:2).
The developed decalcifying protocols enable correlative imaging of the same sample, with preserved intravascular contrast agent, followed by classical histological evaluation [@doi:10.1038/srep41842].
The distinct differences in the X-ray attenuation of µAngiofil and the bone tissue allow us to assess vascularization and bone growth without the need for repeated tomographic scans (before and after decalcification) and subsequent registration.
This saves machine and labor time, and does not damage the sample composition.

The microangioCT approach we present here can be used to simultaneously visualize hard and soft tissues and their associated microvasculature in small and large animal models.
This approach has noteworthy potential applications, such as the simultaneous visualization of bone microarchitecture and microvasculature of bone metastases in a murine xenograft tumor model (Fig.
@fig:3).
This method is especially important for studying bone metastatic models, since it allows for the correct assessment of the microvasculature without decalcifying the sample.
Decalcification can hinder the thorough assessment of pathological processes in bone tissue, which can lead to skeletal-related-events and are associated with shortened survival and deterioration of quality of life [@doi:10.1016/j.jbo.2018.10.004].
Our method also allows for the assessment of the response to specific treatments, such as bone-targeted and antiangiogenic therapies, within the same sample and in 3D.
Additionally, if desired, these results can be followed up with a histological examination [@doi:10.3390/cancers14143443].
This method could offer additional insights into the interplay between angiogenesis and bone growth, bone lysis, and bone turnover, which have yet to be fully elucidated.
The results of this approach will be crucial for selecting potential drug candidates and making proper treatment decisions and efficacy predictions.

In dental research, preclinical models can be divided into small and large animals.
Small animal models, particularly mice and rats, are highly popular, due to their practical size and cost-effectiveness.
The microCT scan of correspondingly small samples perfused with µAngiofil can be performed with a voxel size around 1 µm, providing excellent detail resolution (Fig.
@fig:4).

The small size of hemimandible samples and availability of transgenic lines makes it possible to use a recently published imaging approach [@doi:10.1111/cpr.12578] to visualize peri-implant tissues and microvasculature in small animal models.
However, this tissue-clearing-based approach has some limitations.
Differential shrinkage between soft and hard tissues can lead to anisotropic distortion in samples with both types of tissue and a metal implant.
Furthermore, the maximum visible depth is limited to around 800 µm in a mouse model [@doi:10.1111/cpr.12578], making it impracticable for use in large animal models.

Our presented microangioCT approach does not have such limitations and can be easily applied for visualization of hemimandible and its vascularization in a large animal model like Göttingen minipig (Fig.
@fig:5).
µAngiofil has noticeably different attenuation than mineralized bone and allows for distinct distinction, segmentation and visualization of soft tissue, bone tissue, vessels filled with the contrast agent as well as metal implants according to their gray values in the histogram (Fig.
@fig:6).

To the best of our knowledge, ours is the first study demonstrating non-destructive 3D imaging of the microvasculature of bone in the proximity of metal objects/implants in a large animal model.

### Limitations of our approach

The use of a contrast agent is a potential solution to overcome this limitation, as it has been shown to improve the visibility of the microvasculature [@doi:10.1016/j.vph.2018.09.003; @doi:10.1007/s00429-020-02158-8; @doi:10.1152/ajprenal.00099.2017; @doi:10.1038/srep41842].
However, this approach also has its own limitations, such as the need for an additional contrast agent injection and the potential toxicity of the contrast agent [@doi:10.1016/j.vph.2018.09.003; @doi:10.1007/s00429-020-02158-8; @doi:10.1152/ajprenal.00099.2017; @doi:10.1038/srep41842].

The most significant limitation of the suggested approach is the correlation between resolution and sample size when using microCT imaging.
If the sample is only a few millimeters in diameter, voxel sizes of around 1 µm can be used to visualize the microvasculature down to the capillary bed [@doi:10.1016/j.vph.2018.09.003; @doi:10.1007/s00429-020-02158-8; @doi:10.1152/ajprenal.00099.2017; @doi:10.1038/srep41842].
However, if the sample is larger, like a minipig hemimandible, the achievable voxel size is approximately 10 times larger

Nonetheless, the presented approach is, according to our knowledge, the best what can be achieved with the modern microCT scanners and is a big step forward for qualitative and quantitative imaging in tissue engineering and implantology research as well as many other related research fields.
